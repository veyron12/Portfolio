APPLY PAN

Applying for a PAN card is difficult due to limited digital access, long travel to service centres, and complex paperwork. Customers often lack guidance, leading to delays and rejections. We wanted to enable local retailers to assist with digital PAN applications — making the process simple, accessible, and trusted within the community.

Goal

GOAL

Redesign the in-app service to simplify the PAN application journey by providing clear instructions, a strong visual hierarchy for options, and contextual guidance that helps users and retailers make better choices — ensuring faster completion and higher trust in the service.

Identifying the Problem

The UI did not highlight the main action, presented too many choices in scattered ways, and lacked guidance — making it hard for users to decide and proceed with confidence.

Weak Hierarchy
Lack of Guidance
Split Action Point
Too Many Options
My Role

MY ROLE

Streamline the experience by emphasising the primary action, simplifying and grouping choices, and adding assistive cues — so retailers can navigate the process smoothly and make better decisions while serving their customer.

What We Made

These components support users throughout their journey, helping them understand what the service provides and where they are in the process. They reduce confusion through timely prompts and validations, while reinforcing trust and giving users a sense of progress and achievement.

CLAIM
SETTLEMENT

Customers struggle with claim settlement because the process is complex, unclear, and managed by insurers. Without clear guidance on steps, documents, and connections, they face confusion, delays, and a lack of confidence while filing claims.

Goal

GOAL

A clear and supportive claim journey that explains the process to retailers and their customers. The focus was on simplifying complex insurance steps into an easy-to-understand flow — ensuring that even though the insurer manages the actual booking and settlement, retailers can confidently guide customers with clarity and reassurance.

My Role

MY ROLE

To create an experience that demystifies the claim process for retailers and their customers. My responsibility was to design a structured, step-by-step flow with clear communication touchpoints — making the journey feel simple, transparent, and trustworthy.

Wireframes

Creating early iterations on all possible solutions and navigations that users might need.

What We Made

A simple layout that lets retailers understand all settlement processes across categories — a stepwise process with options to view all ongoing settlements and their status. Building motivation by showing how much insurance is being settled from the platform to build trust and confidence.

PAYNEARBY · 2022
INSURANCE · B2C

GROUP
PERSONAL
ACCIDENT

A micro-insurance product providing protection in case of accidental death or disability, offering a lump-sum payout to cover income loss. Distributed through PayNearby's retail network, it targets underserved communities with affordable access at local shops.

Goal

GOAL

Make the product simple, clear, and quick to adopt. Explain benefits in plain language, reduce decision fatigue with clearer plan options, and streamline steps so retailers could guide customers effortlessly — making the experience accessible for low-literacy users.

Identifying the Problem

The old journey was difficult due to unclear value communication, complex plan options, scattered steps, and needing to fill half the form before seeing plan benefits — causing friction and drop-offs.

Weak Hierarchy
Delayed Benefit Visibility
Redundant Inputs
Long Form
My Role

MY ROLE

Evaluate the existing journey, identify gaps, and create a simpler clearer flow. Make plan details more transparent, reduce form complexity, and design an experience easily explained and adopted at the last mile.

Improving Experience & Interface

BENEFITS SHOWN UPFRONT

Users can now view plan benefits immediately, allowing them to make an informed decision first — rather than filling forms before knowing the value.

DECISION BEFORE DETAILS

The flow was reversed: users decide whether they want to buy, and only then are asked for personal details — reducing hesitation.

REMOVAL OF REDUNDANT STEPS

Earlier, users had to provide the same information multiple times. In the new flow, all required details are captured once.

BACKEND DEDUPLICATION

Any duplicate requirements between the insurer and PayNearby are managed in the backend — users don't experience repeated data entry.

OTP VERIFICATION AT THE END

OTP was earlier placed in the middle, adding friction. It was moved to the end of the journey, making the process smoother and faster.

RETAILER EARNING SECTION

A new section shows retailers how much commission they earn from bookings — building motivation, transparency, and trust.

MEMBER
DETAIL VIEW

A total revamp. The old experience was a disorganised split view — a member list on the left, details on the right — with no proper label mapping. You couldn't tell which card belonged to what until you clicked in, and there were no empty states to guide you when data was missing.

HERO IMAGE — BEFORE / AFTER
Identifying the Problem

The split view forced users to hold context in their head — the list and the detail panel competed for attention, cards weren't labelled clearly, and missing data simply showed blank space. Finance managers managing 50+ members had no scannable way to find who needed attention.

Disorganised Split View
No Label Mapping
No Empty States
Poor Scannability
IMAGE — OLD SPLIT VIEW
What We Made

WHAT WE MADE

We replaced the split view with a simple list → drill-down pattern built on progressive disclosure. The list gives you status and the essentials at a glance; clicking in reveals full detail in layers — cards, limits, transactions — each properly labelled with clear status indicators. Empty states now explain what's missing and what to do about it, instead of showing nothing.

IMAGE — NEW LIST VIEW
IMAGE — DRILL-DOWN DETAIL
Outcome

OUTCOME

The member flow went from a screen the team worked around to one we lead with. Long-standing friction around card visibility resolved after release, and the list → drill-down pattern became the reference for similar views across the product. The qualitative shift was obvious within weeks.

MY
TRANSACTIONS

Redesigning the My Transactions experience to be clearer, more accessible, and easier to use — balancing a clean, modern look with functionality like notes, receipts, categorisation, and search/filter. Built to work across desktop and mobile and handle large datasets smoothly.

Design Audit — Transaction List

The list forced users to scan every line — truncated merchant names, low-contrast dates, amounts with no visual weight, the unclear 'Dr' terminology, and no search, filter, or sort to fall back on.

Truncated Names
Weak Hierarchy
Unclear 'Dr' Term
No Search / Filter
Design Audit — Transaction Details

In the details view, merchant name and amount weren't prioritised, labels were redundant, the card number broke standard banking patterns, 'NA' felt unfriendly, and key actions — upload, notes, raise dispute — didn't look actionable. The whole hierarchy felt flat.

Flat Hierarchy
Redundant Labels
Hidden Actions
Unfriendly 'NA'
Problem Statement

PROBLEM

Users of the My Transactions screen struggle to quickly find, understand, and manage their expense records — important details and key actions are not visually prioritised, leading to confusion, reduced trust, and slower management.

How might we design the transactions list and details view so critical information stands out clearly and reduces cognitive load?

How might we make it faster for users to search, filter, and manage transactions so they can complete key tasks with minimal effort?

The User

THE USER

Employees using corporate cards for business expenses — hotel bookings, rides, meals — who need to review their spends, upload receipts, and add context for compliance.

Wireframes — Transaction List

LIST ITEM

Explored multiple row layouts focusing on scannability, hierarchy, and efficiency — shortened merchant names for faster recognition, visible category for association, and the amount highlighted with a proper debit indicator. The selected layout puts the strongest emphasis on amount, the primary element users scan for, while keeping merchant name and category in balance.

CONTROLS

For managing large datasets: a search bar covering merchant, amount, and tag; filters for date, amount, type, and category; and sorting. Priorities were decided using red routes — search first (fastest path, covers most use cases), then new-to-old sorting, amount, type, category, and location last.

Wireframes — Transaction Details

DETAILS

The details view follows a natural mental model: how much → where & when → from which card → prove it → explain it → fix if wrong. Merchant name, amount, and date sit upfront for instant validation; receipt is prioritised above notes for compliance; secondary actions stay visible but unobtrusive. Plus navigation to move between transactions without going back.

Final Wireframe
Visual Language
Component Library

Every state designed — icons, merchant categories, buttons, approval statuses, filter chips, search, dropdowns, list rows, notes, and receipt uploads — including empty, default, and filled states.

Final UI — Split View

SPLIT VIEW

List on the left for fast scanning, details on the right for instant context — ideal for finance reviewers processing many transactions. Reviewers move through items without losing list context or filter state, bulk workflows get easier, and the master/detail structure makes summary vs. deep detail obvious.

Explorations

ALTERNATES

An alternate full-panel filter view — simple and effective for small datasets, but limited for multi-month selection and real-time results. And a dropdown (expandable row) layout — quick inline access, but it cluttered the list and didn't scale for compliance tasks. The split view won on both counts.
